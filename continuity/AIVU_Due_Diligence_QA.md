## Q1 — Is the greybox analysis process circular or self-fulfilling?

**The question, as a reviewer would put it:**

> *To what extent is your greybox analysis process circular, or self-fulfilling? You generate synthetic trajectories with a forward physics chain, you fit those trajectories with an inverse chain that uses the same physics, and you score the recovery against the parameters you put in. What does that actually demonstrate about a real home?*

**The answer:**

Closed-loop recovery against `aivu_corpus` is structurally circular, and that property is named explicitly in §10.2. The forward chain (`aivu_physics` Phase 1 v4.0 + `aivu_dynamic` v0.2) generates the trajectories, and greybox's inverse fit recovers parameters from those trajectories. What §10.2 actually demonstrates is *internal consistency*: that the inverse code finds its way back to the parameters the forward code put in. It does not demonstrate that the forward chain accurately models a real Phoenix home. We do not claim that it does.

If the forward chain has a structural error — say, the latent-side RealCycle treatment misrepresents how a real coil dehumidifies, or the ERV effectiveness model gets the heat-recovery physics wrong — the closed-loop test passes beautifully while the live-pilot fit produces a posterior that is precisely identified and physically wrong. Closed-loop testing is blind to forward-chain misspecification by construction. Every measurement architecture that includes simulation-based validation has this property. Pretending otherwise would be worse than acknowledging it.

What breaks the circularity is three structural properties of the architecture, not the closed-loop test.

**First, the live pilot itself.** The Phoenix home's actual telemetry is the one input to greybox that is not generated by the forward chain. The §6 Phase D residual is the explicit held-out-data check: Phases A+B+C produce a posterior; Phase D's trajectory is *predicted* from that posterior; the residual between prediction and observation is the diagnostic. On synthetic data, Phase D residuals are determined by integrator noise and parameter degeneracies. On live data, Phase D residuals are also determined by every way the forward chain fails to match physical reality. The first time greybox runs on Phoenix telemetry, that gap becomes visible. Phase D is built to surface it, not hide it.

**Second, the dual-track architecture.** Envelope and HVAC are characterized independently. The HVAC is calibrated against directly measurable quantities (capacity, EER) on Day 3 using equipment-level instrumentation that doesn't depend on the envelope model being right. The envelope is then characterized on Days 4-5 against a *calibrated* HVAC, not a nameplate-specified one. The two tracks can fail differently — and they have to agree at the boundary (the supply-side enthalpy that the equipment delivers equals what the envelope dynamics consume). When they disagree, the disagreement localizes the error. This is what the compound-fracture diagnosis in the Architectural Distillation is structurally about: the industry today couples envelope and HVAC errors into a single black box where neither can be detected; AIVU separates them so each can be measured against ground truth independently.

**Third, the §8 identifiability flags propagating into signed records.** §8 emits per-parameter identifiability flags (prior-only test, posterior-tightness against expectation, joint-identifiability ridge detection, posterior-prior KL divergence) as load-bearing metadata on every signed posterior. A consumer downstream — a Clearinghouse-customer insurer, a warrantor, an external verifier — sees exactly which parameters were constrained and which were not. The architecture does not commit to identifying everything, and it does not hide what it failed to identify. Negative-result information is preserved, not suppressed.

The right way to read §10.2's pass criteria is "the inverse machinery works as designed against the model we have," not "the model is right." These are two separate claims. Greybox's spec-internal tests can only validate the first; the pilot validates the second. The architecture is designed for that division of labor.

The real risk isn't that the circularity exists — closed-loop testing on a forward chain is standard practice and unavoidable. The risk is treating closed-loop pass as evidence of pilot success rather than as evidence of internal coherence. The Phase D held-out residual check, the dual-track boundary reconciliation, and the propagating identifiability flags are how the architecture turns the pilot from confirmation-of-the-model into first-measurement-of-where-the-model-is-right-and-where-it-isn't. None is sufficient alone; together they constitute the structure that makes model-vs-reality gaps legible and load-bearing.

This is the SDH precedent at the architectural level. SDH did not win by being theoretically superior to the asynchronous digital hierarchy. It won by making the cost of the incumbent measurable for the first time. The structural property AIVU shares with that move is that the measurement architecture has to be capable of producing surprises — disagreements between prediction and observation that are legible, load-bearing, and signed. A measurement framework that can only confirm what it already assumes is exactly the polished-set-of-documents-about-a-pilot failure mode. The pilot's value lives in the gaps closed-loop testing cannot see. AIVU's value lives in the architecture that makes those gaps visible.

So: yes, closed-loop recovery against `aivu_corpus` is circular. That circularity is bounded, named, and tested for what it can test. What can't be tested in closed-loop is what the pilot is designed to measure — and the architecture is built to make those measurements legible rather than to suppress them.
